# The Impact of Remote Work on Urban Housing Markets

## Introduction

The rapid adoption of remote and hybrid work arrangements since 2020 has fundamentally altered the geography of housing demand across the United States. As millions of workers gained the flexibility to decouple their place of residence from their place of employment, housing markets experienced a redistribution of demand, price surges, shifting rental preferences, and a wave of adaptive reuse projects converting obsolete office space into housing. This report synthesizes verified findings on how remote work has reshaped urban housing markets, from migration patterns and price dynamics to supply-side responses and equity concerns.

## Migration and the Geographic Redistribution of Demand

Between 2020 and 2022, remote work enabled a substantial redistribution of the population, as workers relocated from expensive urban centers to suburbs, exurbs, and secondary cities ([EIG](https://eig.org/remote-work-and-housing-markets/), [Own Luxury Homes](https://www.ownluxuryhomes.com/markets/national/market-intelligence/remote-work-impact-suburban-home-values)). This shift has been described as the most rapid redistribution of housing demand in modern real estate history ([EIG](https://eig.org/remote-work-and-housing-markets/), [Own Luxury Homes](https://www.ownluxuryhomes.com/markets/national/market-intelligence/remote-work-impact-suburban-home-values)). More broadly, the freedom to work remotely allowed workers to move away from expensive coastal cities toward secondary cities, suburban areas, and even rural communities in search of lower costs and better amenities ([Uglydeals](https://uglydeals.com/real-estate/how-remote-work-is-reshaping-housing-demand-and-migration-patterns/), [FTC Publications](https://news.ftcpublications.com/core/how-remote-work-is-reshaping-rural-communities-globally/)). This pattern is corroborated by evidence of increased net migration toward suburbs and smaller cities since 2020 ([Philadelphia Fed](https://www.philadelphiafed.org/-/media/FRBP/Assets/working-papers/2025/wp25-36.pdf), [U.S. Census Bureau](https://www.census.gov/library/stories/2024/05/exurbs-city-population.html)), and migration itself is considered a significant factor in evaluating the welfare impacts of remote work on residential sorting ([Philadelphia Fed](https://www.philadelphiafed.org/-/media/FRBP/Assets/working-papers/2025/wp25-36.pdf), [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0014292125002454)).

Secondary markets offering affordable housing, good schools, and outdoor amenities saw substantial population inflows as a direct result of this remote-work-enabled mobility ([Society Science Array](https://society.sciencearray.com/remote-work-reshaping-cities-communities-suburbs), [Ahlend](https://ahlend.com/the-rise-of-secondary-markets-where-to-invest-beyond-major-metros/)). A notable example is Tulsa's Remote program, which has attracted over 3,000 households since 2018 ([Society Science Array](https://society.sciencearray.com/remote-work-reshaping-cities-communities-suburbs), [Upjohn Institute](https://research.upjohn.org/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1055&context=up_technicalreports)); economist Timothy Bartik estimates that between 58 and 70 percent of these moves would not have occurred without the program's incentive ([Society Science Array](https://society.sciencearray.com/remote-work-reshaping-cities-communities-suburbs), [Upjohn Institute](https://research.upjohn.org/up_technicalreports/50/)). However, this influx has not been without costs: rising real estate prices in rural and small-town communities can price out existing residents or create shortages of affordable housing ([FTC Publications](https://news.ftcpublications.com/core/how-remote-work-is-reshaping-rural-communities-globally/), [White House](https://www.whitehouse.gov/research/2025/06/the-deterioration-of-housing-affordability-in-rural-america/)), and some rural communities are struggling to expand infrastructure quickly enough to meet new demands on utilities, schools, and health services ([FTC Publications](https://news.ftcpublications.com/core/how-remote-work-is-reshaping-rural-communities-globally/), [Improve Workspace](https://improveworkspace.com/challenges-for-rural-areas-adapting-to-remote-workers/)).

## Price and Rent Effects

The migration wave coincided with a sharp national escalation in housing costs. Real house prices rose by more than 20 percent between 2020 and 2022 ([EIG](https://eig.org/remote-work-and-housing-markets/), [Realtor.com](https://www.realtor.com/news/trends/here-are-the-u-s-cities-where-home-prices-rose-and-fell-the-most-in-2022/), [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0304405X23001265), [Econofact](https://econofact.org/why-and-where-are-housing-prices-rising)), while real rents rose by roughly 8 percent over the same period ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0304405X23001265), [iPropertyManagement](https://ipropertymanagement.com/research/average-rent-by-year)). These changes were not evenly distributed geographically, but varied considerably by region ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0304405X23001265), [Harvard JCHS](https://www.jchs.harvard.edu/blog/rents-have-soared-across-country-home-prices-grew-even-faster)). Research attributes a large share of this national price growth directly to remote work: the shift to remote work explains over one-half of the 18.9 percent increase in U.S. real house prices between 2019 and 2023 ([NBER](https://www.nber.org/papers/w30041), [KDVR](https://kdvr.com/news/data/yes-it-is-remote-workers-who-spiked-housing-rent-costs/181/)). Remote work increased overall housing demand in part by creating a need for dedicated home offices and by increasing the amount of time people spend at home ([EIG](https://eig.org/remote-work-and-housing-markets/), [Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco](https://www.frbsf.org/research-and-insights/publications/economic-letter/2022/09/remote-work-and-housing-demand/)). Workers used their newfound flexibility to search for cheaper housing, lower tax rates, and more desirable amenities—including warmer climates and more space—which contributed to home price increases nationally ([BLS](https://www.bls.gov/opub/mlr/2023/beyond-bls/remote-work-to-blame-for-rise-in-housing-prices.htm), [NBC News](https://www.nbcnews.com/politics/economics/remote-workers-left-housing-havoc-created-remains-rcna68874), [Penn IUR](https://penniur.upenn.edu/publications/policy-brief-how-remote-work-is-affecting-real-estate-markets), [PNAS](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.2408930121)).

Within cities, remote work altered the traditional spatial structure of housing prices by flattening intracity house price gradients—narrowing the steep difference that historically existed between urban-core and outlying-area prices ([EIG](https://eig.org/remote-work-and-housing-markets/), [Journal of Regional Science](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/jors.12693)). This represents a broader revaluation of real estate, with declining office rents in major cities accompanying rising residential prices outside them ([Penn IUR](https://penniur.upenn.edu/publications/policy-brief-how-remote-work-is-affecting-real-estate-markets), [Volcker Alliance](https://www.volckeralliance.org/sites/default/files/2023-01/Real+Estate+Economics+-+2022+-+Van+Nieuwerburgh+-+The+remote+work+revolution++Impact+on+real+estate+values+and+the+urban.pdf)). Looking further ahead, projections suggest that in the long run, shifting location demand tied to remote work will actually decrease rents overall by 0.3 percentage points, with the negative effect concentrated most heavily in cities that were initially the most expensive ([EIG](https://eig.org/remote-work-and-housing-markets/), [NYU Urban Lab](https://urbanlab.nyu.edu/the-short-and-long-run-effects-of-remote-work-on-u-s-housing-markets/)). Underlying these shifts is the scale of remote work adoption itself: more than half of American workers—roughly 70 million people—can perform their jobs remotely ([Lincoln Institute](https://www.lincolninst.edu/publications/articles/2023-05-office-residential-conversions-are-on-the-rise/), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9988592/)), and 22 percent of workers are expected to work entirely offsite in future years ([Lincoln Institute](https://www.lincolninst.edu/publications/articles/2023-05-office-residential-conversions-are-on-the-rise/), [Upwork](https://www.upwork.com/press/releases/upwork-study-finds-22-of-american-workforce-will-be-remote-by-2025)). Surveys similarly project that, long-term, 20 percent of full workdays will be supplied from home, compared to just 5 percent pre-pandemic ([Penn IUR](https://penniur.upenn.edu/publications/policy-brief-how-remote-work-is-affecting-real-estate-markets), [BFI](https://bfi.uchicago.edu/wp-content/uploads/2020/12/BFI_WP_2020174.pdf)).

## Office Vacancy and the Rise of Office-to-Residential Conversions

The flip side of falling demand for centralized workspace has been a historic surge in office vacancy, which in turn has spurred a growing wave of office-to-residential conversions. Nationally, the office vacancy rate surpassed 17 percent in the fourth quarter of 2022, up from 12.1 percent in late 2019 ([Lincoln Institute](https://www.lincolninst.edu/publications/articles/2023-05-office-residential-conversions-are-on-the-rise/), [Colliers](https://knowledge-leader.colliers.com/u-s-national-research/u-s-office-market-q4-2019-a-solid-close-to-the-year/), [Lincoln Institute PDF](https://www.lincolninst.edu/app/uploads/2024/04/home-work-lla230704.pdf)). Some markets were hit especially hard: San Francisco's office vacancy rate rose from just 4.8 percent before the pandemic to 27.3 percent by the end of 2022 ([Lincoln Institute](https://www.lincolninst.edu/publications/articles/2023-05-office-residential-conversions-are-on-the-rise/), [SocketSite](https://socketsite.com/archives/2022/10/office-vacancy-rate-in-san-francisco-just-hit-a-new-high/), [The Real Deal](https://therealdeal.com/san-francisco/2026/07/12/sf-homes-sell-1m-over-asking-as-office-boom-flows-south/)).

This glut of underused office space has fueled adaptive reuse at a scale unseen before the pandemic. Nationally, conversion activity had been less than 1.2 million square feet annually before 2020, but reached 4.1 million square feet through August 2025—an increase of roughly 3.4 times over five years ([IntricGlobal](https://intricglobal.com/en/insights/articles/us-office-residential-conversions-2026), [First American](https://blog.firstam.com/cre-insights/why-office-conversions-havent-moved-the-market...yet), [Facilities Dive](https://www.facilitiesdive.com/news/nyc-zoning-changes-high-vacancy-rates-drive-office-conversion-activity/802120/)). By the start of 2026, the national office-to-residential conversion pipeline had reached 90,300 apartment units, a 28 percent year-on-year increase ([IntricGlobal](https://intricglobal.com/en/insights/articles/us-office-residential-conversions-2026), [Smart Cities Dive](https://www.smartcitiesdive.com/news/office-to-apartment-conversion-projects-rentcafe/816098/), [Construction Dive](https://www.constructiondive.com/news/office-to-apartment-conversion-projects-rentcafe/816315/)), building on the 70,700 apartment units projected from conversions across 2025 alone ([IntricGlobal](https://intricglobal.com/en/insights/articles/us-office-residential-conversions-2026), [RentCafe](https://www.rentcafe.com/blog/rental-market/market-snapshots/adaptive-reuse-office-to-apartments-2025/)).

New York City exemplifies this trend at the local level. As of the first quarter of 2025, the city had 44 completed, ongoing, and potential conversion projects totaling 15.2 million gross square feet ([NYC Comptroller](https://comptroller.nyc.gov/reports/office-to-residential-conversions-in-nyc-economics-and-fiscal-estimates/), [6sqft](https://www.6sqft.com/nycs-first-wave-of-office-to-residential-conversions-could-create-over-17000-new-homes-report-says/)), with the potential to produce approximately 17,400 new apartments citywide ([NYC Comptroller](https://comptroller.nyc.gov/reports/office-to-residential-conversions-in-nyc-economics-and-fiscal-estimates/), [JPMorgan](https://www.jpmorgan.com/insights/real-estate/community-development-banking/office-to-residential-conversion-what-to-know)). Within Manhattan south of 59th Street alone, 12.2 million gross square feet of conversion projects contain 14,500 apartments ([NYC Comptroller](https://comptroller.nyc.gov/reports/office-to-residential-conversions-in-nyc-economics-and-fiscal-estimates/), [Ranola Real Estate](https://ranolarealestate.com/nyc-developers-8-weeks-14500-apartments-467m-deadline/)).

## Shifting Renter Preferences, Urban Form, and Planning Responses

Remote work has not only shifted where people want to live but also what they want from their housing. Work-from-home arrangements have reduced demand for residential properties in dense urban centers while increasing demand for properties in less dense, more affordable suburban and rural areas ([Penn IUR](https://penniur.upenn.edu/publications/policy-brief-how-remote-work-is-affecting-real-estate-markets), [BLS](https://www.bls.gov/opub/mlr/2023/beyond-bls/remote-work-to-blame-for-rise-in-housing-prices.htm)), and more broadly, the demand for urban density and job proximity has fallen with the advent of remote and hybrid work ([Zillow](https://www.zillowstatic.com/bedrock/app/uploads/sites/42/2023/11/Zillow-Rentals-Consumer-Housing-Trends-Report-2024.pdf), [McKinsey](https://www.mckinsey.com/mgi/our-research/empty-spaces-and-hybrid-places-chapter-1)). This is likely to produce more dispersed urban settlement patterns over time ([Penn IUR](https://penniur.upenn.edu/publications/policy-brief-how-remote-work-is-affecting-real-estate-markets), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11551397/)), even as remote work creates a mismatch between where residential units are located and where jobs remain concentrated ([Journal of Law and Economics](https://academic.oup.com/jla/article/15/1/201/7261639), [Morgane Richard](https://morganemrichard.github.io/morganerichard.com/Morgane_Richard_JMP.pdf)), reflecting the broader dynamic of labor becoming untethered from centralized workplaces ([Stanford Law](https://law.stanford.edu/wp-content/uploads/2022/04/Online-Edition-Stern.pdf), [SSRN](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3800412)) and dispersing geographically ([Oxford Academic](https://academic.oup.com/jla/article/15/1/201/7261639), [NBER](https://www.nber.org/papers/w29181)).

At the household level, tenants in 2024 have increasingly sought larger rental homes with dedicated office spaces and reliable high-speed internet to support remote work ([Rent Magazine](https://rentmagazine.com/the-ongoing-impact-of-remote-work-on-the-rental-market-shifts-in-demand-and-landlord-strategies/), [Ross Companies](https://www.ross-companies.com/blog/2024/11/11/the-impact-of-remote-work-on-renter-preferences-and-property-management/), [The Multifamily Journal](https://themultifamilyjournal.com/high-speed-internet-and-reliable-cell-reception-are-top-priorities/)). This shift in preferences has driven increased demand—and rising rental prices—in suburban and rural areas as landlords adjust to accommodate remote workers seeking more space and amenities ([Rent Magazine](https://rentmagazine.com/the-ongoing-impact-of-remote-work-on-the-rental-market-shifts-in-demand-and-landlord-strategies/), [The Realty School](https://therealtyschool.com/market-trends-and-insights/the-return-of-suburban-demand/)).

These changes have prompted calls for significant reform in urban and regional planning. Localities are being urged to rewrite zoning laws that have historically separated work and home, moving toward transit-focused planning better suited to remote work realities ([Stanford Law](https://law.stanford.edu/wp-content/uploads/2022/04/Online-Edition-Stern.pdf), [American Planning Association](https://www.planning.org/publications/document/9275792/)). Specific modifications are needed in areas such as planning, licensing, parking, signage, and noise regulations to accommodate the growing "home-business creep" ([DVM Artin Law](https://www.dvmartinlaw.com/remote-works-surprising-impact-on-real-estate-and-local-zoning-laws/), [The CGO](https://www.thecgo.org/research/zoning-for-opportunity-a-survey-of-home-based-business-regulations/)). More broadly, urban planning must adapt to increased demand for mixed-use developments that support remote work ([Improve Workspace](https://improveworkspace.com/policy-implications-of-remote-work-for-urban-and-regional-planning/), [Urban Land Institute](https://urbanland.uli.org/capital-markets-and-finance/reimaging-mixed-use-districts-at-uli-fall-meeting-placemaking-long-term-thinking-show-the-way)), with zoning reforms viewed as essential for accommodating flexible workspaces in suburban and rural areas as well ([Improve Workspace](https://improveworkspace.com/policy-implications-of-remote-work-for-urban-and-regional-planning/), [Smart Growth America](https://www.smartgrowthamerica.org/zoning-matters-in-rural-areas-too-why-rural-communities-must-consider-zoning-reform/)). City planners and policymakers are likewise being called upon to reshape infrastructure investments in response to these shifting patterns ([Multidisciplinary Journal](https://www.allmultidisciplinaryjournal.com/uploads/archives/20250529115348_MGE-2025-3-185.1.pdf), [US Resist News](https://www.usresistnews.org/2024/07/02/remote-work-urban-impact/)), and to rethink urban planning strategies more fundamentally to move away from traditional office-centric development models ([Multidisciplinary Journal](https://www.allmultidisciplinaryjournal.com/uploads/archives/20250529115348_MGE-2025-3-185.1.pdf), [Nagoya Repository](https://nagoya.repo.nii.ac.jp/record/2010179/files/k14950_thesis.pdf)).

## Equity and Distributional Concerns

The benefits of remote-work-driven housing mobility have not been shared equally. Lower-skilled, less-educated, and lower-income workers are less likely to have access to remote work opportunities, which places them in direct competition for housing with higher-income, highly educated workers relocating into the same areas ([Philadelphia Fed](https://www.philadelphiafed.org/the-economy/regional-economics/the-geographic-and-economic-implications-of-working-from-home), [ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S014362282300019X)). Because remote workers tend to be higher-income earners, the pandemic-era shift to remote work has exacerbated income inequality, amplifying disparities between income groups ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0927537123001379), [PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12366878/)). Workers with remote access have been able to search for cheaper housing and more desirable amenities by relocating to less expensive areas, engaging in a form of geographic arbitrage unavailable to those without remote work access, who remain unable to capture similar benefits or enjoy the same freedom to relocate ([BLS](https://www.bls.gov/opub/mlr/2023/beyond-bls/remote-work-to-blame-for-rise-in-housing-prices.htm), [Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco](https://www.frbsf.org/research-and-insights/publications/economic-letter/2022/09/remote-work-and-housing-demand/)).

## Conclusion

Remote work has reshaped urban housing markets through several interconnected channels: it triggered an unprecedented redistribution of housing demand toward suburbs, exurbs, and secondary cities; it contributed substantially to the national surge in house prices and rents between 2020 and 2022 while flattening the price gradient between urban cores and their peripheries; it left major cities with historic levels of office vacancy, catalyzing a growing wave of office-to-residential conversions in cities like New York and San Francisco; and it has driven new tenant preferences for space and connectivity that are pushing planners to rethink zoning and mixed-use development. At the same time, these shifts have generated real strains—rising costs for existing residents in once-affordable secondary and rural markets, and an uneven distribution of benefits that favors higher-income remote-capable workers over those without such flexibility. Taken together, the evidence indicates that remote work has not merely shifted where people live, but is actively restructuring the economic geography of American housing markets, with policy and planning responses still catching up to the pace of change.
